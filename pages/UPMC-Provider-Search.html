UPMC Provider Search

A search tool to find a
doctor.

Roles

UI/UX, Interaction Design, Researcher

Duration

Jan. 2020 - Apr. 2020

Tools

Sketch, UserZoom, HTML, CSS, JS

Only 3% of our members will use the filter feature, and end up calling our help desk to find a doctor.

UPMC Provider Search is a search tool for UPMC’s millions of members. With over 50,000 unique visitors monthly and over 3,000,000 unique visitors as of March 2020, We know that our members need to discover new doctors, nurse practitioners and health providers alike. I was brought on the project with a fellow UX designer on the team to address usability issues and improve our users to become more powerful in their search.

Many Users,
Many Skill Levels.

Who's experience can you fix if your userbase is fragmented?

We have a wide variety of users. Expectedly, we see members of all different backgrounds (commercial members, Medicare, Medicaid, SNP, CHIP, etc.), but we also see our own customer support team reference our public directory when people call not knowing where to look for a provider. Finally, even providers and doctors will reference our data to ensure it is up to date with new locations, contact info and much more. While there are many different types of users, we know our largest group is our health insurance members, but our true power users are our customer service representatives. This was a problem since every call we receive from members on this topic cost UPMC money.

Houston, We Have
Many Problems.

No product is perfect, but we had some serious usability issues.

Since every call will cost UPMC time and money, we wanted to improve our interface to better improve our search functionality and bring a friendlier approach to searching. A few problems upon researching with users and analyzing our analytics on the site included:

  • Our header and footer bury the important content that users care about
  • Empty results lacked empathy and feedback to help a user
  • Only 3% of users applied even a single filter to their search
  • Warning and error messaging was not clear and eventually left users calling our customer support for clarification
  • The page interaction was confusing to older members and two scrollbars was jarring

Upon assessing all these problems, we knew that tackling all of them at once would be difficult, and had gone back to the drawing board to assess all of our problems within one “how might we” statement to have an ultimate goal to reach.

How Might We...

Collect all of our problems into one statement? Ludicrous!

The problems our users are facing are mostly interaction problems, and the outcomes are not what they expected. What we did was then sat down with power users, our customer service representatives, to see how they work and where most users calling in fail. All our problems we outlined were true (and even more so than we expected). This brought us back together with the business leadership to come up with an overarching problem statement of: “How might we construct a predictable search experience that caters information to users’ needs?” Along with this, we tied a few metrics to our success including: system usability score (SUS), net promoter score (NPS) and percentage of users filtering results.

Learning
The Product.

Mapping and validating and mapping and validating and mappi...

Because I joined this product with a fellow UX designer that was already focused 100% on the product, I needed to familiarize myself and the rest of the team with key moments in a member journey, and where we can ultimately aim for in the next few sprints. I mapped each user’s experience based on many different starting places and hosted several brainstorming sessions with the product owner, project manager and the other designer to validate where we can solve the problems I stated earlier.

Sketch,
Test,
Repeat!

"I love polarizing results, it means our design can grow."

Upon coming to the conclusion of the places we need to improve the product in the user flow, I began diving deep into ideation of certain problems including our content in the card, the scroll interface, the filter ability, error messages and empty state. Individually attacking each usability problem, we pushed an A/B test virtually and got back results from users during certain tasks performed. Many showed an improvement, but my favorite results were polarizing results that varied across the board. It was an invitation to pivot our thoughts in that part of the design and recreate new designs.

"One Off"
Cases.

No all users are the same.

However, we also know not every search result returns a result. However, different users have different options to take based on their medical plan status. For instance Medicaid members may not have the same resources as commercial members, and we needed to design each of these pages to ensure we were correct in our design handoffs for engineers. While not the most important step, we wanted to ensure the handoff was as streamlined as possible for engineers.

Pitching to Team Leaders

How can pitching to stakeholders be more engaging? Make it feel real!

To help improve the vision for stakeholders and engineers, I coded a prototype in HTML, CSS and JS and worked closely with the engineering team to help set deadlines based on the complexity of the build. While I wouldn’t suggest doing this for every project I am on, this product was seeing massive changes that were all validated by users and need to be as close as possible to the prototypes we tested with users.

Key Takeaways
& Constraints.

Duly noted.

While we had accomplished shipping a product that was better tested and met the metrics we set early on, there were a few notes we left the product owner for the upcoming work to be done on the product. Our SUS score increased from a D score of 56 when I joined to a B score of 75 upon finishing these updates. We also saw NPS increase from 32% to 52% and filter usage increase from 3% to 24%. Upon viewing these results, we knew that we still had room to improve, but found success in our member market by empowering them with a better experience.

However, we also knew there were some additional product design tasks to tackle next. First, there was more research needed to be done on language. We want to use words that are easily understood, but also understand that insurance is a confusing and regulated industry. We also worked on other features in the results cards that we currently did not have in our dataset we gathered everything from. With these in mind, we began the next journey attacking these hard pressed topics.
