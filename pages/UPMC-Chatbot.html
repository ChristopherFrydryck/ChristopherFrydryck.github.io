UPMC
Chatbot

Research and usability study for a healthcare chatbot.

Roles

UI/UX, Researcher, Facilitator

Duration

Sept. 2019

Tools

Sketch, InVision, UserZoom

If it was this quick and easy to get the information, I would [use a chatbot].

In September of 2019, I was involved in a two week research and design sprint called "Airplane Mode." The thought was to turn your phone off or on airplane mode in the afternoon and focus solely on research and design to bring back to our team. Unlike Google Venture's Design Sprints, research into the problem had already be addressed by other team members and business stakeholders. It was our job to test with our health plan members how they interact with a Chatbot.

By the end of day one, we came to the conclusion that our objective is: "how might we improve the self-service experience for our members using a chatbot?"

Preflight
Check.

Yes, get ready for cheesy airplane jokes.

Before this sprint was ready for takeoff, several of us on the team had been in some way or another involved with the future initiative of chatbots at UPMC Health Plan. From prior research to designing the future vision of the company's digital presence, the team was selected by a group of leaders who wanted to expediate the work.

From our prior knowledge, we knew that chatbots had some foundational UX pieces that were key to testing. Chatbots should be explicit about what they are and disclose they are not a real human. They should be short and concise with their answers. The chat pattern should be familiar and look somewhat like a messaging app. And lastly, you should be able to talk to a real person with the press of a button.

The
Research
Plan.

Let's get down to business.

We knew the next step before we dove into testing was geting the plan together. We wanted to rapidly iterate and make changes for the following day of testing. This process involved four tests with an average of 5.25 participants per usability study.

From the research we conducted, we knew a few key differentiators between the great and the mediocre experiences was that the chatbot is trustworthy, actionable and convenient to use. If this triad is not met in a chatbot, it will ultimately flop and users will not use it.

These differentiators helped us identify what we needed to test and to construct a usability study that would answer the burning questions we have for our members.

Usability
Testing.

Test. Iterate. Test. Iterate. Test.

Because of the short timespan between the beginning of the project and when it concluded, we had to run tests in the evening, synthesize them in the morning and redesign in the afternoon. We were laser focued around the clock on making sure we were testing the right thing with the right user.

We continued this iterative design approach for five days, and expediated some of the process using UserZoom to help save us time in the data collection phase. Our final test was in person with real members of UPMC Health Plan, and we gathered their feedback on the design. During my time facilitating, I asked one member if they could see themselves using the chatbot more than our current tech offerings, and they exclaimed, "If it was this quick and easy to get the information, I would."

Our
Findings.

Oh, and we also suggested more research. How very UX of us.

We had presented our findings to nearly 20 leaders of all different teams that are heavily invested in the future of chatbots. Limitations aside, we had fairly concrete evidence that users trust chatbots, they like the idea of using one and they are more convenient than our alternative solutions.

Some areas we could continue our research would be the chat on-boarding experience, the general chat language, the transfer between chatbot and human and testing with older Medicare members. The foundation we set for the next designer or researcher to continue diving deeper is well established, and we are sure that the future testing of chatbots at UPMC has room to grow.
