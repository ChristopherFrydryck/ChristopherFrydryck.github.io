Christopher “Fred” Frydryck is a firm believer in capturing the only means of perfection: natural wildlife. Sadly, over the years, this nature is far and few between. Over commercialization, population growth and ignorance to what is natural have been ongoing issues that previous conservation photographers such as Ansel Adams or Edward Weston had never encountered.

Since the turn of the century, a rapid increase in human exploration to unknown territory has been prevalent due to the increase of technology. Automobiles, GPS, mobile phones and much more have made exploration a breeze for the modern day Lewis and Clark. This is what Frydryck likes to call “human intrusion.” The act of altering nature and forbidding it from ever reaching the utmost perfect state because of human interaction.

This human intrusion can create abstracism in nature, however nature sometimes defies what humans could establish on a canvas. This inspired Frydryck to capture these obscure abstract tendencies in nature. Christopher exclaims,

“I love photographing things that abstract in nature. Abstractism has always been associated with human creation, and I find it unfair to nature that it has been disassociated with such obscure beauty in modern society."

For now, Frydryck has travelled to the southwestern United States to study conservation photography, and intends to continue further human intrusion photographs through eastern Canada and the Midwestern national parks of the United States.
