Orchid Design System

A design system for all.

Roles

UI/UX, Testing, Prototyping

Duration

Dec. 2018 - Present

Tools

Sketch, HTML, CSS, React
Design systems should value context even more than consistency. Using the right component at the right time can give our users a more predictable experience.

Orchid is UPMC Health Plan's design system that includes everything from color and typography, to pattern designs for components including tables, toggle switches, dropdowns and much more.

My role on the design system team is to read the guidelines and work with the visuals to make the UI kit for the Sketch library inclusive of all functions and states via the overrides panel in Sketch. I also have performed component QA and work closely with our dedicated React developers to hand off the visuals and code specs. We also hold meetings to go over questions, comments or concerns on team members' pattern designs.

The current state of Orchid is focused on web app development, with future consideration into the native app space.

The
Users.

Early before I jumped straight into designing, I had to understand "who am I designing the UI kit for?"

Design systems are beneficial to everyone, but early into taking the role of lead UI kit designer, I knew I had to understand my users. Luckily, they were sitting all around me. I put together a test plan to see how our designers would react to new interactions in the designs. From implementing min/max lines in a component to adding margin and padding overrides, I tested with five designers of all different backgrounds and proficiencies in Sketch.

When the study concluded, we discovered that users found padding helpful to ensure that the component spacing inside the element was accurate, but found margins confusing or unnecessary. Also, novice designers felt they needed to follow the exact margin size, which was an unexpected consequence of providing too many guides. We ultimately avoided any override component guides that handcuffed users, and only carried padding into future pattern designs.

Pattern
Tree.

Now that I understood users' preferences, I needed a compass to guide me in the build process.

Before jumping into building, I look at the guidelines for the component provided by a team member and further understand the complexity of a component. Some patterns such as a card may lack any proper overrides in Sketch, while other patterns like toggle switches or dropdowns may have different designs and state values.

Luckily, my coding background and understanding of component prop values helps me create a mapped diagram of how a pattern should function. In some instances, I like to map out a physical diagram of the component states. This helps me ensure that all use cases of our designers are met without needing to return to a component later.

Building
Blocks.

We know our users and our component architecture. Let's get groovy.

Once I have concluded my architectural build of a component, I will begin working in Sketch. I will continue to use the map I made of the component along with the write-up from my team member as reference to ensure the build encompasses all the needs of the users.

When designing, the team and I need to ensure that the designs are both reminiscent of similar patterns, but also they nest well together and compliment each other. For instance, inline links and text need to nest well within each other without clashing visually.

Considering also that some patterns are similar to something that already exists, such as a multi-select dropdown and a normal dropdown, I need to ensure that the overrides and naming conventions are uniform across all patterns. Attention to detail is key, and having one pattern size noticably larger than others or the default size is mobile when everything else is desktop is important for our designers.

The
Handoff.

What good is handing work to someone without proper documentation?

Once the build in Sketch is complete, I create a separate artboard that goes over specs, and if need be, I will sit down with whoever is developing the component to go over a gameplan for the props, CSS modules, etc. I'll upload the document to Zeplin to share with any developer that needs it as a reference document for future projects.

Our design system is coded in React and then transpiled into Angular for some of UPMC's web projects. Luckily, I have the development experience in these frameworks to sit down often with developers of all different projects that are implementing Orchid and help assist them in integrating their codebase to Orchid. We are currently in the process of adding more components to the design system, and improving our documentation for developers to access a public npm.

Wrap Up.

What next? No, I don't just twiddle my thumbs...

We try our best to push this process from idea to live design as fast a possible. During this time we have bugs (of course). I am always on call to fix issues for the team and talk with developers when they run into issues or have a question about a component.

I am on the first project at UPMC that is adopting Orchid, and since then multiple product and project managers have expressed interest in Orchid taking place in their work.
